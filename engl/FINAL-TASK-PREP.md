Joseph Merrick, also known as "the elephant man", was an English man who became famous in the late 19th century due to his extreme physical deformities as a freak show performer. Born in 1862 in Leicester England, he lived to be 27 before passing in 1890. His medical condition is still unclear but he is believed to have suffered Proteus syndrome, a rare disability that leads to unnatural growth of bone and other tissues. Merrick became well known because of his unusual appearance and was shown in freak shows across England. Even though he faced many difficulties throughout his life, people admired him for his kind personality and strong character, making him famous even to this day in media and culture.
Joseph Merrick was born into a family of five. He later perpetuated a legend claiming that, while he was still in the womb, his mother was nearly trampled by an elephant, an event he believed caused his severe deformities. This story contributed to the nickname by which he would become widely known: The Elephant Man. Although Merrick was born without any apparent health complications, signs of his condition began to emerge around the age of two, and his physical deformities quickly became more pronounced. After the death of his mother when he was eleven, Merrick’s father remarried. His new stepmother, viewing him as a  "monster," refused to care for him. By the age of twelve, Merrick was forced to find work to support himself. However, his worsening condition made it difficult to keep a job. He briefly worked as a door-to-door salesman, but frequent mockery over his appearance eventually made him to quit. With few options left, Merrick was placed in a poorhouse. His life changed when he was discovered by the manager of a freak show, who saw in him a potential for the show.
Joseph Merrick’s career began in the early 1880s, when he joined a traveling freak show.  At the time, people with physical differences were often exhibited to the public as curiosities. Merrick wore a long coat and a large hat with a cloth to cover his face. When the time came, he would slowly remove the cloth to reveal himself. Many people came to see him, not just out of fear or shock, but also because they were curious about the man behind the appearance.
He was polite and gentle, and this surprised many visitors. Even though he was shown as part of a freak show, Merrick always tried to act with calm and dignity. His manager brought him to cities around England, and for a while, the show gave him money and food. When "freak shows" were forbidden in England, the presenter took his in Europe, but Merrick was abandoned by him and was left with nothing.
His life was changed again when, getting back to London, a doctor named Frederick Treves took him in by curiosity for the man. Merrick no longer worked in shows, but he still received many visitors who had heard of “The Elephant Man.”
The Royal London Hospital published a raise for fund in the times newspaper to provide for the housing needs of "The Elephant man", and thanks to the support queen Victoria brought, he was housed at the Hospital for the rest of his life. During his time at the Royal London Hospital, Joseph Merrick finally found peace and safety. He no longer had to live on the streets or be part of a freak show. The hospital gave him his own room, and the doctors and nurses treated him with care and respect.
At the hospital, Joseph had the chance to do things he enjoyed. He liked reading books, writing, and making detailed models made out of cardboard. Many important people came to visit him. They were surprised by how kind, polite, and intelligent he was, despite his appearance.
Sadly, Joseph's health slowly got worse. On April 11, 1890, he died at the age of 27. Doctors believe he died because he tried to sleep lying down, which he usually could not do because of the size and weight of his head. His body could not support that position, and he likely suffocated in his sleep.
His skeleton was kept at the hospital for medical research, and in recent years, the rest of his remains were buried in a private ceremony.
Joseph Merrick was undoubtedly a very intelligent and kind person, a one-of-a-kind man who went through many unjustified hardships caused by his unfortunate condition. Although animalised and objectified during his time as a freak show performer, he remains remembered as an example of a great and strong person.
